TXT Project

A page dedicated to the text analysis project of Digit-100.

For this project, I decided to look into the State of the Union Addresses of the presidents near John F. Kennedy's term in office. The texts were split into two groups: One including JFK's addresses and the three presidents prior to JFK's death, and the other including the three presidents following JFK's death.

To begin my research into the topic, I decided to look for differences between how the two corpuses handled the idea of war as compared to the world. I found that the presidencies before JFK's death (including his term itself) included much more mentions of war in the context of the world. The presidencies following JFK's death however, mention war less often when talking about the world. Could this be the result of a unification of the people? Maybe the assasination of JFK was a turning point in how violence was seen by the people? During his presidential term, JFK aimed to be more transparent with how politicians address the public. One of JFK's main focuses in his term was his drive to put a man on the moon. The cold war didn't officially end (if you can say it did at all) until 1991, but the mention of war signidicantly diminished in the following union addresses. Through the idea of the space race, it's possible that tensions between the United States and the Soviet Union were alleviated in non-violent means.

I found it interesting that JFK rarely mentioned the soviets at all. It seemed to be an issue of importance to other presidents near his term, but JFK avoids mentioning the soviet union itself in his State of the Union Addresses. Could this be a reason for his assasination? Maybe he was killed for his lack of attention to global conflict on the soviet front? During this time, the United States and the Soviet Union was engaged in the Cold War. While this was happening, the soviets were also invading the middle east. This was an issue of great importance on the global stage. I am of the opinion that the true highest level of authority in the United States lies with the CIA. It's possible that the CIA deemed the issue of the Cold War as too important to ignore. The lack of mention on the topic could have contributed to a lack of expected military sign-ups, which the CIA might have seen as harmful to the nation. It is also possible that his reluctancy to mention Soviets may have served as a de-escalation method! I have particular opinions on how the media portrays violent information, as I believe violence to produce more violence. Maybe JFK understood this, and wanted to prevent such an infohazard from reaching the American population?

His death did seem to have an impact on how the globe wanted to proceed with the Cold War, as evidenced by the diminished presence of the word in the addresses following JFK's death. If this is truly the case, it might be a consideration that JFK's assasination was a point of climax in the Cold War. The fear of futher violence may have caused a push towards a less violent media. It's likely that this trend continued for several presidential terms following JFK, and reflected in the State of the Union Addresses.
