TXT Project

A page dedicated to the text analysis project of Digit-100.

For this project, I decided to look into the State of the Union Addresses of the presidents near John F. Kennedy's term in office. The texts were split into two groups: One including JFK's addresses and the three presidents prior to JFK's death, and the other including the three presidents following JFK's death.

To begin my research into the topic, I decided to look for differences between how the two corpuses handled the idea of war as compared to the world. I found that the presidencies before JFK's death (including his term itself) included much more mentions of war in the context of the world. The presidencies following JFK's death however, mention war less often when talking about the world. Could this be the result of a unification of the people? Maybe the assasination of JFK was a turning point in how violence was seen by the people?

I found it interesting that JFK rarely mentioned the soviets at all. It seemed to be an issue of importance to other presidents near his term, but JFK avoids mentioning the soviet union itself in his State of the Union Addresses. Could this be a reason for his assasination? Maybe he was killed for his lack of attention to global conflict on the soviet front? During this time, the United States and the Soviet Union was engaged in the Cold War. While this was happening, the soviets were also invading the middle east. This was an issue of great importance on the global stage.

This screenshot makes it clear that the subject of war is much more prevalent with the presidencies prior to JFK's death. It also shows how reluctant JFK was to talk about war in his addresses himself. His death did seem to have an impact on how the globe wanted to proceed with the Cold War, as evidenced by the diminished presence of the word in the addresses following JFK's death.

Also of note, the popular 3grams compared between the collection of texts. A lot of similar phrases appear between the two corpuses, making it noted that the rhetorical phrases were not altered across this portion of time. Strangely on the left, money is a focus for president Truman only. On the right, the Soviet Union makes its appearance in 3grams, showing that the phrase is more densely spoken of following JFK, going against the idea of war being less of a pressing issue. Maybe this was in an attempt to lower tension between the US and the Soviet Union?
